a letter I didn’t send

I keep a sentence open like a door that never stops swinging.
I keep a blank space where your name could rest,
but my mouth keeps choosing softer verbs—
miss, care, cherish—
as if tenderness can be measured by what I refuse to say.

Some words feel too bright for the room I live in.
Some words arrive with a weight I can’t carry in public.
So I practice in the margins:
a quiet like that pretends it’s enough,
a careful hold dear folded into my pocket,
a private long for I hide behind jokes.

Still, the word I want keeps moving when I reach for it.
It slips into the corner of the page, it turns away—
not cruelly, just impossibly—
like a hand that won’t be held unless you stay.

If I am honest, I have been learning the grammar of us
through small repetitions, through waiting, through return.
I choose you in ordinary moments—
in the way my thoughts circle back,
in the way silence still keeps your shape.

And maybe that is how the hardest word becomes real:
not by being clicked, not by being proven,
but by being lived long enough to stop running.

So here it is, written where it can’t interrupt anyone—
a word like a held breath, a word like a promise:
love.
